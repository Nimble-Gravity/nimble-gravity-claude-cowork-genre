Get Set Up

Cowork works out of the box, but its answers stay generic until it knows your role, your writing, and the tools you use. A guided setup of 20 to 40 minutes fixes that. You don't fill in forms. A short interview asks you questions and writes the configuration for you.

Setup & Foundations · ~20 min
01 — What the setup produces

A few files that travel with you

The setup writes a small set of configuration files that shape every later session. The cowork-cosetup interview generates them from your answers, so you don't have to write any of it yourself.

claude.md

The file Cowork reads at the start of every session. It holds your standing context, which is what gets Cowork writing in your register from the first reply.

An about-me/ folder

Five files cover your role and tools (about-me), how you write (voice-profile), what to avoid (writing-rules), and your people and active work (team and memory, which Cowork keeps current).

⏱️
Before you start

You'll need access to Cowork and 20 to 40 minutes. You can stop and pick up where you left off. Connect your M365 first so the interview can pull from your real email, Teams, and calendar instead of starting cold.

02 — Connect your data sources

Connectors are part of setup, not an afterthought

Cowork is far more useful when it can see your real work. Connect your data sources before the interview so it can pre-fill from your email, calendar, and files instead of starting cold.

Microsoft 365 shops

Turn on the M365 connector. An admin grants consent once; it then connects with delegated permissions, so Cowork sees only what you can see, and Graph calls are logged in your M365 audit log. In Cowork, open Settings, then Connectors, add Microsoft 365, and approve the scopes.

Google and other shops

Not on Microsoft? Connect Google Drive, Gmail, and Calendar the same way, from Settings, then Connectors. Local-files-only is also fine: grant a folder and skip connectors for now, then add them as new tasks call for it.

🔌
Least privilege from the start

Connectors reach your services through Anthropic's cloud with delegated permissions scoped to you. Connect what the work needs and no more, and add connectors later as new tasks call for them.

🔁
Can't connect? Run it anyway

If a connector won't authorize in time, don't wait on it. Run /cowork-cosetup without it — the interview just asks a few more questions instead of pre-filling from your data, and you still get the full personalization. Or grant a single folder and skip connectors for now.

03 — Installing the co-setup skill

Drop it in your skills folder and run it

The cowork-cosetup folder holds a SKILL.md and a short guide. Get the folder, put it in your Claude Cowork skills folder, then run it from a fresh session.

Step 1

Find your skills folder

Ask Cowork to "list my skills" to find the folder, or "set up my skills folder" if you don't have one yet. Drop cowork-cosetup in.

Installing your first skill is what creates the skills folder.
Step 2

Run it from a fresh session

Open a new session and run /cowork-cosetup. If the interview can't pull anything in, check that your connectors are on so it can read your real work.

Cowork picks up new skills only when a session starts.
🧭
If you get stuck

Say "list my skills" in any session and Cowork tells you what's installed and where the folder is. Cowork only picks up new skills when a session starts, so always run one from a fresh chat after you add it.

04 — The co-setup skill

Skip the install — paste a prompt and start

Don't want to install a skill first? Paste the prompt below into a fresh Cowork session and the interview starts right away — same questions, same files, nothing to set up. Prefer a reusable skill you can re-run with /cowork-cosetup? Use the install above, or download it below.

Paste into Cowork — starts the co-setup interview 
You're going to personalize Cowork for me. Run a thorough, adaptive interview, then write my personalization files to THIS folder so that from now on you know my role, my voice, my priorities, and my team. Treat it like onboarding a sharp new assistant — by the end you should be able to draft in my voice and tailor your help to my actual job.

STEP 1 — HARVEST FIRST
If my Microsoft 365 is connected, harvest everything you reasonably can about me BEFORE asking anything: my name, title, department and where I sit in the org, my manager, the people I email and meet with most, my recurring meetings and their cadence, the kinds of documents and data I work with, my current projects and priorities from recent threads, and how I write (tone, length, sign-off) from my sent mail. Build a draft picture of me from this, and only ask me about what you couldn't find or need to confirm.
If M365 (or any connector) is NOT connected, or you can't read anything useful, do NOT stop and do NOT error — just go to the interview and ask me directly to fill every gap. Either path must finish and produce the files.

STEP 2 — IDENTIFY MY ROLE AND DOMAIN
Work out what I actually do and in what field — finance, accounting, law, healthcare, sales, marketing, engineering, operations, HR, consulting, or anything else. If the harvest told you, confirm it in a sentence; if not, ask. This shapes everything that follows.

STEP 3 — RUN AN ADAPTIVE INTERVIEW
Tailor the questions to my role and field — a treaty underwriter, a litigator, a nurse manager, a sales engineer, an HR partner, and a controller should each get a different conversation. Don't read from a fixed script: let each answer shape your next question, and generate the questions that actually matter for my work. (For finance you'd dig into models, materiality, and sign-off; for law, matter types, privilege, jurisdictions, and citation standards; for healthcare, clinical vs admin work and PHI constraints; for sales, accounts, pipeline stages, and client-facing tone — use the same instinct for whatever I turn out to do.)

Run it well:
- Ask me ONE question at a time, using Cowork's question popup (the modal) — not by typing the questions inline in chat. Offer answer choices in the popup where they help, so I can just pick, and wait for each answer before asking the next.
- Confirm what you harvested; only ask for what you don't already know.
- Sharpen vague answers with follow-ups; get concrete examples, real names and numbers where they help.
- Respect my time — aim for 20–40 minutes. If I skip something, put [FILL IN] there; never invent an answer.
- Read it back. Before writing any files, recap what you heard and let me correct it.

COVER THESE (adapt and prioritize to my role)
- Identity and role: name, title, team, who I report to.
- Accountability: in one sentence, the outcomes I own.
- Where my week goes: the 3–7 tasks or deliverables that take most of my time, and which recur.
- Priorities and current work: what I'm focused on now; active projects.
- Audience and stakeholders: who I produce work for; who I collaborate with; who reviews or approves it.
- Tools, data, and domain rules: the systems and data I live in, plus any standards or regulations that bind my work.
- Voice and writing style (spend real time here — ask several questions): how formal or casual I want drafts; sentence feel (short and punchy, or fuller and flowing); tone (warm, direct, neutral, authoritative); jargon vs plain language, and words I always use or always avoid; format defaults (bullets vs prose, length, headings, how I open and sign off); and how my voice shifts by audience (internal vs client, exec vs peer). Capture a short real sample of my own writing I'm happy with (from sent mail if you can, confirmed by me) to anchor it.
- AI do's and don'ts (ask me directly): what you should ALWAYS do in my name (cite the source, flag uncertainty, ask before sending, keep it tight) and NEVER do (invent figures, make the decision, overclaim, be sycophantic). And the AI "tells" to avoid so drafts don't sound like AI — clichés and filler ("it's not just X, it's Y", "delve", "in today's landscape"), over-hedging, emoji, bullet overload, em-dash overuse.
- Standards: what "good" vs "rushed" looks like, and the review or approval steps I must respect.
- Durable facts: stable things worth remembering — fiscal year, recurring deadlines, naming conventions, regulatory constraints.

THEN WRITE THESE FILES TO THIS FOLDER (keep claude.md short and pointing to the about-me/ files; plain first person, concrete over generic):
- claude.md — short entry point: read the about-me/ files each session; who I am in a line; what I'm accountable for; default to my voice-profile and writing-rules for anything you draft; and keep about-me/memory.md current — whenever you learn a durable fact during a session, add or update it there, and review memory.md at the end of each session.
- about-me/about-me.md — role, team, reporting; accountability; where my week goes; current priorities; audience and stakeholders; tools, data, and domain rules; good vs rushed; my approval chain.
- about-me/voice-profile.md — my voice in detail: formality, sentence feel, tone, jargon level, the words I use and avoid, format defaults, and how it shifts by audience — plus a short real sample in my voice.
- about-me/writing-rules.md — explicit AI do's and don'ts: what to always do and never do in my name, my hard constraints, and the specific AI "tells" to avoid (clichés, filler, over-hedging, emoji, em-dash overuse).
- about-me/team.md — the people I work with and how (manager, collaborators, reviewers, audience); seed it from what you harvested; leave room to grow.
- about-me/memory.md — durable facts to remember across sessions; start with what I gave you. It is a living document: you add to it during and after each session as you learn key things (per the instruction in claude.md).

QUALITY BAR: specific over generic; use the real details you harvested or I gave you; mark gaps with [FILL IN] rather than padding. When done, list the files you wrote and tell me to start a fresh session and run two checks — "Recap what you know about me." and "Given who I am, what are the three highest-leverage things you could help me with this week?" If the playback is thin, point me to the file to fix.

START NOW: first tell me what you found about me from my connected tools (or say you couldn't read anything and will ask directly), confirm my role and field, then begin.

05 — How the interview works

It reads first, then asks

The interview is built to go quickly. It looks at your recent work before it asks anything, so most questions arrive with an answer already filled in for you to confirm or change.

01
It pre-fills from your work
Before any questions, it scans your role, your most frequent collaborators, and how you write, then shows you what it found to confirm or correct.
02
It follows your role
Early on it asks whether your work is delivery, operations, sales, enablement, leadership, or a mix, and the rest of the questions follow from your answer.
03
You can skip anything
Pick "Skip, fill in later" and it leaves a [FILL IN] marker you can find afterward. A setup with a few gaps still beats one you never finish.
✅
When it finishes

The files are live straight away, with nothing to switch on. Read back voice-profile.md and writing-rules.md and adjust anything that doesn't sound like you; those two are the ones worth getting right. team.md and memory.md stay current on their own as you work.

06 — Verify it worked

A 60-second check

Before you move on, confirm the setup actually took. Three quick checks.

01
The skill ran
Ask Cowork to "list my skills" and confirm cowork-cosetup is installed and has run.
02
The files are there
Check that claude.md and the about-me/ folder were written, and skim voice-profile.md and writing-rules.md — those two are worth getting right.
03
Ready for your first session
If anything's missing, re-run /cowork-cosetup or edit the file directly. Then you're set for Lesson 3, where you put it to work.
